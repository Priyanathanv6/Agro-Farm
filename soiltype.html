Types of soil in India

Soils of India

The soils of India are classified based on various criteria, including their physical and chemical properties, as well as their agricultural significance.

The Indian Council of Agricultural Research (ICAR) has developed a comprehensive classification system that categorizes Indian soils into eight major groups:

  1. Alluvial
  2. Black
  3. Red
  4. Laterite
  5. Desert (arid)
  6. Forest
  7. Peaty
  8. Saline

Alluvial Soil

Alluvial soil is the most extensive and agriculturally significant soil type in India. It is formed by the deposition of sediments brought down by rivers from the Himalayas and other highlands. It is mainly found in the Indo-Gangetic-Brahmaputra plains, covering states like Punjab, Haryana, Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, and West Bengal. It is also found in the coastal plains of peninsular India.

  • Rich in potash, phosphorous, and lime.
  • Highly fertile and ideal for diverse crops.
  • Easily workable with minimal inputs.
  • Supports rabi and kharif crops.
  • Prone to flooding during heavy rains.

Crops: Rice, Wheat, Sugarcane, Cotton, Tobacco, Pulses, Oilseeds, and Jute.

Black soil

Black Soil (Regur Soil)

Black soil, or Regur, is found in the Deccan Plateau across Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, Andhra Pradesh, and Tamil Nadu. It originates from volcanic rocks and is rich in clay.

  • Excellent moisture-retention.
  • High clay and nutrient content.
  • Cracks in dry seasons help aeration.
  • Best for cotton cultivation.

Crops: Cotton, sugarcane, tobacco, wheat, pulses, citrus fruits.

Red Soil

Red soils cover Tamil Nadu, Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, and parts of Maharashtra. They are derived from metamorphic rocks and are rich in iron.

  • Well-drained and porous.
  • Low in nitrogen and phosphorus.
  • Easy to cultivate with fertilizers.

Crops: Cotton, groundnut, tobacco, pulses.

Laterite soil

Laterite Soil

Laterite soils are found in regions with high rainfall and alternate wet-dry cycles. Found in Western & Eastern Ghats and northeastern states, it forms due to leaching.

  • Rich in iron and aluminum.
  • Acidic and low in fertility.
  • Supports plantation crops with manure.

Crops: Tea, coffee, cashews, rubber.

Desert (Arid) Soil

Desert soil is sandy and found in Rajasthan, Gujarat, and parts of Haryana and Punjab. It is poor in organic content and requires irrigation for cultivation.

  • Low water retention.
  • High salt content.
  • Needs organic manure and irrigation.

Crops: Barley, cotton, wheat, millets.

Desert soil

Forest soil

Forest Soil

Found in hilly areas of Jammu & Kashmir, Himachal, Uttarakhand, and Western Ghats. Its fertility varies with altitude.

  • Acidic at high altitudes.
  • Fertile loamy soil at lower altitudes.
  • Rich in organic matter.

Crops: Tea, coffee, spices, wheat, maize.

Saline soil

Saline Soil

Found in arid regions like Gujarat, Punjab, Uttar Pradesh, and coastal belts. High salt content makes it infertile unless reclaimed.

  • Hinders plant growth.
  • Requires gypsum and drainage for improvement.

Crops: Barley, cotton, sunflower, rice.
